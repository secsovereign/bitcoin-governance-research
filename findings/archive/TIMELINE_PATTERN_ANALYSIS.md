## Pattern 4: Governance Failures Precede Major Forks

### The Sequence

**Chronological sequence of events reveals governance failures before forks:**

1. **2010**: Inflation bug (CVE-2010-5139)
   - Early governance failure
   - No formal process to prevent

2. **2013**: Version 0.8 split
   - Chain split due to governance issues
   - No formal consensus mechanism

3. **2015**: Blocksize wars begin
   - **Preceded by**: High external pressure (453 emails in 2015)
   - **Preceded by**: Years of informal governance (2011-2014)
   - **Result**: Major governance conflict

4. **2017**: SegWit activation and Bitcoin Cash fork
   - **Preceded by**: Peak external pressure (493 emails in 2017)
   - **Preceded by**: Blocksize wars (2015-2017)
   - **Result**: Hard fork due to governance failure

5. **2018**: Inflation bug (CVE-2018-17144)
   - **Preceded by**: Post-fork consolidation
   - **Result**: Same type of bug as 2010 (no learning)

6. **2022-2024**: Luke Dashjr security incident and maintainer privilege revocation
   - **2022**: Security breach (PGP key compromise, 200+ BTC stolen)
   - **2024**: Maintainer privileges temporarily revoked as precaution, later reinstated
   - **Context**: Highest external pressure year (522 emails in 2022)

### The Pattern

**Governance failures consistently precede major conflicts:**

- **Informal governance** → **External pressure** → **Major conflict** → **Fork/removal**
- **No formal processes** → **Vulnerability to pressure** → **Governance crisis**
- **No learning** → **Same mistakes repeated** (inflation bugs in 2010 and 2018)

### The Implication

**The sequence reveals a causal pattern:**
1. Informal governance creates vulnerability
2. External pressure exploits vulnerability
3. Major conflicts result from lack of formal protections
4. No learning from past failures

**This is not isolated incidents - it's a pattern of governance failure.**
